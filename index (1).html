Chapter 01

The Genesis: ARPANET

1960s — 1980s

It started in the midst of the Cold War. The year was 1969 — the same year humans walked on the Moon. Deep inside UCLA, a team of scientists sent the first message across a network called ARPANET. The message was supposed to be "LOGIN." The system crashed after just two letters: "LO."

And yet, those two letters changed everything. Funded by the U.S. Department of Defense, ARPANET was designed to survive a nuclear attack — a network with no single point of failure. By 1971, it connected 15 nodes. By 1973, it had crossed the Atlantic.

In 1983, the network adopted TCP/IP — the universal language that still powers the internet today. Then in 1989, a physicist named Tim Berners-Lee at CERN wrote a proposal: "Information Management: A Proposal." His boss scribbled on it: "Vague, but exciting."

Chapter 02

The Wild West: Dot-Com Boom

1990s — Early 2000s

The World Wide Web went public in 1991, and the world was never the same. Suddenly, anyone with a modem could publish to the entire planet. The sound of dial-up became the anthem of a generation — that glorious screech of connecting to something bigger than yourself.

Then came the gold rush. Between 1995 and 2000, the NASDAQ surged 400%. Companies with no revenue and absurd names raised billions. Pets.com, Webvan, Boo.com — they burned bright and fast. Everyone wanted a piece of the digital frontier.

But amidst the chaos, giants were born. Amazon started selling books from a garage. Google was a Stanford research project. eBay proved strangers could trust each other online. When the bubble burst in 2000, it wiped out $5 trillion — but the survivors would reshape civilization.

Chapter 03

The Connected Age: Social Media

2003 — 2012

If the dot-com era built the roads, the social era filled them with people. In 2003, a site called Myspace let you design your own corner of the internet (usually with terrible fonts and auto-playing music). Then a Harvard student named Mark Zuckerberg launched Facebook from his dorm room in 2004.

YouTube arrived in 2005 — "Broadcast Yourself." Twitter followed in 2006 with a radical idea: say it in 140 characters. The internet was no longer something you visited. It was something you lived in. Your identity, your relationships, your memories — all uploaded to the cloud.

By 2010, Facebook had 500 million users. The Arab Spring showed social media could topple governments. But darker questions emerged: Who owns your data? What happens when algorithms choose what you see? Can democracy survive the attention economy?

Chapter 04

Internet in Your Pocket: Mobile

2007 — 2020

On January 9, 2007, Steve Jobs walked onto a stage and said: "Today, Apple is going to reinvent the phone." He wasn't exaggerating. The iPhone didn't just change phones — it put the entire internet in your pocket. Within a year, the App Store launched, and a new economy was born.

Instagram (2010), Snapchat (2011), Uber (2009), WhatsApp (2009) — apps that couldn't exist without mobile. By 2016, mobile internet traffic surpassed desktop for the first time. The internet was no longer tied to a desk. It was everywhere.

Streaming killed the DVD. Netflix, Spotify, and podcasts turned every commute into entertainment. The gig economy redefined work. Cloud computing made trillion-dollar companies possible from a laptop. But with great connectivity came great surveillance — and the world began to ask: at what cost?

Chapter 05

The Next Frontier: Web3 & AI

2020 — Beyond

We stand at the edge of another revolution. Web3 promises to return ownership to the people — a decentralized internet where you control your data, your identity, your digital assets. Blockchain isn't just cryptocurrency; it's a new architecture of trust.

Artificial Intelligence has exploded. Models like GPT and generative AI can write, code, paint, and reason. The line between human and machine creativity is blurring. AR and VR are building the metaverse — persistent digital worlds layered on top of reality.

But the biggest question isn't technical — it's human. Will we build an internet that serves everyone, or one that deepens divides? Will AI augment us or replace us? The next chapter of the internet isn't written in code. It's written by the choices we make today.

Era: 2020–Future

Key idea: Decentralized. You own your data & assets.

Who owns it: The users (via blockchain & tokens)

Analogy: A co-op — you build it, you own it.
